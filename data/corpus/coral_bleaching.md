# Coral Bleaching

Coral bleaching occurs when corals are stressed and expel the symbiotic algae,
called zooxanthellae, that live inside their tissues. These algae give corals
much of their colour and, through photosynthesis, supply up to 90 percent of
the energy the coral needs. Without them the coral turns white and begins to
starve.

The most common trigger is elevated sea temperature. A sustained rise of only
about 1 degree Celsius above the usual summer maximum can be enough to start
bleaching. Other stressors include pollution, unusually low tides, and
excessive sunlight.

A bleached coral is not dead. If conditions return to normal quickly, the algae
can recolonise and the coral may recover. But prolonged stress leads to death,
and large-scale "mass bleaching" events on reefs such as Australia's Great
Barrier Reef have become more frequent as ocean temperatures rise.

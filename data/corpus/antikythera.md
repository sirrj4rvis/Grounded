# The Antikythera Mechanism

The Antikythera mechanism is an ancient Greek hand-powered device, often
described as the world's first known analogue computer. It was recovered in
1901 from a shipwreck off the Greek island of Antikythera, and is dated to
roughly the second century BCE.

The device used a complex system of at least 30 interlocking bronze gears to
model the movements of the Sun and Moon through the zodiac. By turning a hand
crank, an operator could predict astronomical positions and eclipses decades
in advance. It also tracked the four-year cycle of the ancient Olympic Games.

The smallest gears have teeth only about a millimetre across. Nothing of
comparable mechanical complexity is known from the following thousand years,
and the level of miniaturisation was not matched again until the astronomical
clocks of fourteenth-century Europe.

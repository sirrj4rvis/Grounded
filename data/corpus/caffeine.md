# How Caffeine Works

Caffeine is the most widely consumed psychoactive substance in the world. It
works mainly by blocking adenosine receptors in the brain. Adenosine is a
molecule that builds up during waking hours and promotes drowsiness; by
occupying its receptors, caffeine prevents that drowsiness signal from being
received, which is why it makes people feel more alert.

After it is consumed, caffeine is absorbed quickly and reaches peak levels in
the blood within about 30 to 60 minutes. Its half-life in a healthy adult is
typically around 5 hours, meaning half the dose has been cleared in that time,
though this varies widely with genetics, pregnancy, and liver function.

Regular consumption leads to tolerance: the brain produces additional adenosine
receptors, so a habitual drinker needs more caffeine for the same effect.
Abruptly stopping can cause withdrawal symptoms such as headaches and
irritability, usually lasting a few days.
